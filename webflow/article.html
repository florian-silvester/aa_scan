Hans Fischer’s father was an “impassioned farmer,” his mother an innkeeper. He has been familiar with physical work and handling materials since he was a boy. “I would have liked to study art, but didn’t know how this would have been feasible.” So he served an apprenticeship with Jörg von Manz from 1978 to 1980. After working with this legendary ceramics artist, who died in 1997, studying was no longer an issue.

Maria Fischer’s story is different. She, too, knew early on that she wanted to work with her hands. “I think I had a vague desire to heal, to get away from a demanding intellectual home, to escape in a way. I didn’t want to make art, I wanted to practice a craft.” In the 1970s, the idea of working autonomously was in the air: of doing something that you could penetrate and master in its entirety, as a conceptual alternative to the division of labor and perhaps alienation. Being able to intertwine work and private life was the decisive factor for Maria Fischer: “I knew that as a potter, I’d be able to have children and work as well, that I could make this ideal of somehow juggling it all a reality, and fulfill this desire of not having to divide myself between work and family, but instead combine it all somehow. This seemed to be possible if I worked as a ceramist, and it was.”

Hans and Maria Fischer first met at the potters’ market in Diessen, and have been living and working in the house of Hans Fischer’s parents for almost 40 years now. Maria Fischer still talks about “being free to do.” It’s the process of doing or creating something that makes her happy. Even if living a holistic life like this has its downsides. “You sometimes ask yourself if you haven’t taken up too many different things and can’t do everything as well as you might otherwise,” says Hans. And Maria adds: “If you span such a wide arc, you don’t have the energy to experiment with a thousand different glazes.”
